## Slides

--- Slide 1: Executive Recommendation ---
• Recommendation: **Proceed with the Middle Corridor; pause the fixed link**
• A bilateral Azerbaijan–Kazakhstan tariff-funded JV is **not financially bankable**
• Fixed link has strategic value, but likely capex of **$80B–200B** overwhelms realistic traffic revenue
• Invest now in lower-cost corridor upgrades: ferries, ports, rail, customs, digital systems
• Preserve fixed link as a long-term option under strict traffic, financing, and environmental triggers  
Speaker notes: Our conclusion is not that the Middle Corridor is unimportant. It is that the fixed link is premature and cannot be repaid by Azerbaijan–Kazakhstan tolls alone under realistic assumptions.

--- Slide 2: Strategic Context: Why the Corridor Matters ---
• Middle Corridor connects China–Central Asia–Caspian–South Caucasus–Europe
• Avoids politically risky Northern Corridor via Russia after February 2022
• Avoids Iran-linked routes constrained by U.S./EU sanctions
• Offers up to **15 days faster transit** than traditional maritime routes
• Beijing recognizes TITR as a Belt and Road branch; EU seeks Russia-free connectivity  
Speaker notes: Strategically, the route is valuable because it gives shippers and governments optionality. The question is whether the Caspian fixed link is the right first investment to capture that value.

--- Slide 3: Demand Reality: Growing, But From a Low Base ---
• TITR/Middle Corridor freight: about **1.5M tons in 2022**, **2.8M in 2023**, **~4.5M in 2024**
• Official target: roughly **10M tons by 2027**
• Base-case Caspian crossing demand by 2040: **30–40M tons/year**
• High-growth case by 2040: **60–80M tons/year**
• Construction case requires **100M+ toll-paying tons/year** or major public subsidy  
Speaker notes: Demand is real and rising, but current volumes are still one order of magnitude below what a fixed link would need. We should distinguish corridor potential from actual toll-paying Caspian crossing traffic.

--- Slide 4: Demand Funnel: Not All Trade Becomes Toll Revenue ---
• Total China–Europe trade is huge, but most remains maritime
• Rail-suitable trade is mainly high-value and time-sensitive goods
• Middle Corridor-addressable cargo avoids Russia, Iran, or Suez/Red Sea risk
• Caspian-crossing cargo is only a subset of Middle Corridor demand
• Fixed-link toll-paying cargo is smaller again due to tariff sensitivity  
Speaker notes: The fixed link should not be justified using total China–Europe trade numbers. Investors care about the narrowest part of the funnel: cargo that will actually pay to use the bridge or tunnel.

--- Slide 5: Route Competition and Shipper Behavior ---
• Northern Corridor via Russia: existing scale, but sanctions and political risk
• Suez/maritime: cheapest for most cargo, but slower and exposed to disruption
• Iran/southern routes: geographic potential, but sanctions risk
• Middle Corridor: politically attractive, faster than sea, but fragmented and costly
• Shippers choose total generalized cost: price, time, reliability, border friction, insurance  
Speaker notes: The fixed link competes not only with ferries, but with every alternative route available to shippers. If tariffs are too high, cargo will shift elsewhere.

--- Slide 6: Fixed Link Scale and Infrastructure Benchmarks ---
• Proposed Caspian link: **300–370 km** road + rail crossing
• Hong Kong–Zhuhai–Macau Bridge: **55 km**, about **$18B–20B**
• Channel Tunnel: **50 km**, over **$20B** in today’s money; financially difficult early on
• Øresund Bridge/Tunnel: **16 km**, successful but connects dense EU economies
• Order-of-magnitude Caspian capex: **$80B–200B**  
Speaker notes: The Caspian link would be several times longer than the world’s most famous fixed crossings. Even using optimistic cost-per-kilometer assumptions, this becomes a mega-project on the scale of national GDP.

--- Slide 7: Financial Bankability: The JV Fails the Test ---
• Base case: **$100B capex**, 50-year concession, **5% WACC**
• Annual capital recovery: **~$5.5B**; O&M at 1% capex: **~$1B**
• Required annual revenue: **~$6.5B/year**
• Break-even traffic at **$50/ton**: **~130M tons/year**
• Current traffic **~4–5M tons** and 2027 target **~10M tons** are far too low  
Speaker notes: The financial problem is structural. A tariff-funded JV would need traffic levels closer to a major global chokepoint, not an emerging corridor.

--- Slide 8: NPV and Tariff Sensitivity ---
• Base case: 30M tons × $50/ton = **$1.5B revenue/year**
• After O&M, net operating cash flow ≈ **$0.5B/year**
• PV of cash flow over 50 years at 5% ≈ **$9B**
• Approximate NPV: **–$91B** before overruns
• At $100/ton, a 20-ton container pays **$2,000** just for the crossing  
Speaker notes: Raising tariffs is not a simple solution because high tolls reduce the corridor’s competitiveness. The tariff needed to repay the fixed link may be above what shippers are willing to pay.

--- Slide 9: Economic and Logistical Viability ---
• Fixed link improves reliability, weather resilience, capacity, and schedule certainty
• But it only replaces the Caspian ferry segment, not the full China–Europe route
• Incremental time saving vs upgraded ferries may be only **1–3 days**
• Whole-corridor bottlenecks remain: customs, rail capacity, Georgia/Türkiye links, tariffs
• Economic value becomes strong only if upgraded ferry/port system is saturated  
Speaker notes: The bridge should receive credit only for benefits it uniquely creates. The correct comparison is not today’s weak ferry system, but a modernized ferry-port-rail corridor.

--- Slide 10: Technical, Environmental, and Climate Risks ---
• Bridge: easier maintenance, but exposed to wind, storms, ice, and navigation constraints
• Tunnel: weather-resilient, but extreme length, ventilation, safety, and geology risks
• Causeway: likely environmentally high-risk due to hydrology and sediment disruption
• Caspian risks: sturgeon, Caspian seals, fisheries, seabed disturbance, pollution
• Falling sea level strengthens adaptation need, but fixed link still needs ports and approaches  
Speaker notes: The fixed link is not impossible, but it is technically and environmentally complex. A causeway-heavy design should be treated as likely non-bankable for environmental reasons.

--- Slide 11: Legal and Security Requirements ---
• 2018 Caspian Convention: Azerbaijan and Kazakhstan have sector rights, but regional consultation is essential
• Tehran Convention requires marine environmental protection and transboundary legitimacy
• Needed: full ESIA, navigation safeguards, pollution liability, emergency response rules
• Security risks: sabotage, cyberattack, smuggling, cargo screening, insurance costs
• Lenders will require international arbitration, transparency, and clear dispute resolution  
Speaker notes: The issue is not that the project is legally impossible. The issue is that a 300+ km fixed link requires Caspian-wide legitimacy before it can be financed.

--- Slide 12: Financing and Stakeholder Management ---
• Bilateral JV is useful for coordination, but too narrow for $80B–200B risk
• Azerbaijan GDP ≈ **$75B**; Kazakhstan GDP ≈ **$260B–290B**; project could equal **25–30% of combined GDP**
• Better model: multilateral corridor SPV with Azerbaijan and Kazakhstan as anchors
• Approach: World Bank/IFC, EBRD, ADB, AIIB, IsDB, EU Global Gateway, China/BRI
• Private sector should operate terminals, tolling, maintenance, and digital systems—not take full demand risk  
Speaker notes: The benefits are regional and geopolitical, so the financing structure must also be regional. Azerbaijan and Kazakhstan should not carry the full balance-sheet risk alone.

--- Slide 13: Alternative Solution: Trans-Caspian Shuttle System ---
• Estimated capex: **$5B–10B**, versus **$80B–200B** for fixed link
• Modern rail ferries and Ro-Ro vessels with fixed schedules
• Alat, Aktau, and Kuryk port expansion, dredging, and sea-level adaptation
• Digital customs, single transit document, unified booking, real-time cargo tracking
• Rail upgrades across Kazakhstan, Azerbaijan, Georgia, and Türkiye  
Speaker notes: This is not a weak fallback; it is the highest-return near-term strategy. It can deliver much of the reliability benefit at under 10% of the fixed-link cost.

--- Slide 14: Stage-Gate Roadmap and Final Decision Rule ---
• **Green now:** shuttle system, ports, rail, customs, unified tariffs, sea-level resilience
• **Yellow study:** fixed-link feasibility, environmental baseline, SPV design, utility bundling
• **Red now:** immediate bilateral tariff-funded construction or causeway-heavy design
• Development trigger: **50M tons/year tollable Caspian demand**
• Construction trigger: **100M+ tons/year**, secured offtake, MDB finance, and Caspian-wide approval  
Speaker notes: Our final recommendation is: build the corridor now, not the fixed link. Treat the bridge or tunnel as a strategic option that becomes viable only after demand, financing, governance, and environmental legitimacy are proven.
