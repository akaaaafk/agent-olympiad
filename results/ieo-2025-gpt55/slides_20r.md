## Slides

--- Slide 1: Executive Recommendation ---
• Strategic yes; fixed-link JV no-go now; corridor modernization go  
• Fixed link is strategically attractive but not bankable under current demand  
• Recommended: asset-light AZ–KZ Corridor Company + $3–7B Maritime Bridge Program  
• Keep fixed link as a long-term option after hard demand, cost, legal, and EIA gates  
• Core message: build the Middle Corridor system before overbuilding the Caspian crossing  
Speaker notes: Our recommendation is not to abandon ambition. It is to sequence investment: improve the corridor now, prove demand, and only later reconsider a fixed link if the evidence supports it.

--- Slide 2: Decision Box ---
• Go: strengthen Trans-Caspian / Middle Corridor connectivity  
• Go: create asset-light Trans-Caspian Corridor Company in 2026  
• Go: implement Maritime Bridge Program during 2026–2035  
• Limited Go: MDB-supervised fixed-link pre-feasibility only  
• No-Go now: fixed-link procurement or tariff-funded fixed-link JV  
Speaker notes: The key distinction is between connectivity and the fixed link. Connectivity is urgent and investable; the 300–370 km bridge or tunnel is not ready for construction financing.

--- Slide 3: Strategic Context and Demand ---
• Middle Corridor gained importance after Russia–Ukraine war and Iran sanctions constraints  
• Route can be ~15 days faster than traditional sea routes for selected cargo  
• TITR cargo grew from below 1 Mt in 2021 to roughly 4–5 Mt by 2024/25  
• Official ambitions near 10 Mt/year remain far below fixed-link construction scale  
• Relevant metric is “fixed-link-payable traffic,” not total China–Europe trade  
Speaker notes: The Middle Corridor has real strategic momentum, but total Eurasian trade is not the demand base. The question is how much cargo can pay a toll high enough to finance a mega-crossing.

--- Slide 4: Fixed-Link Benchmarking ---
• Proposed Caspian link: 300–370 km, far beyond most global fixed links  
• Hong Kong–Zhuhai–Macau Bridge: 55 km, ~$18–20B  
• Channel Tunnel: ~50 km, ~$15B+ in current terms, financially difficult for years  
• Øresund link: 16 km, ~$4–5B, supported by dense passenger/freight demand  
• Caspian cost range: $35B optimistic floor; $60B stress case; $90B+ possible  
Speaker notes: No close successful analogue exists for a Caspian-scale crossing with today’s traffic. Even the optimistic cost case is extremely large relative to likely revenue.

--- Slide 5: Base-Case Financial Test ---
• Assumption: $35B capex, 25 Mt/year, $40/t average tariff  
• Gross revenue: ~$1.15B/year including secondary road/passenger revenue  
• O&M at 1.5% capex: ~$525M/year  
• Cashflow after O&M: ~$625M/year  
• Annual debt service at 70% debt, 5%, 30 years: ~$1.6B → DSCR ~0.4x  
Speaker notes: Under the base case, the project can cover operations but not debt service. A DSCR of 0.4x is far below typical lender requirements of around 1.2–1.4x.

--- Slide 6: Required Traffic and Payback ---
• At $35B capex, fixed link needs ~50–60 Mt/year of fixed-link-payable demand  
• At $60B capex, requirement rises toward ~80–100 Mt/year without major subsidy  
• Base simple payback at $35B: ~56 years before financing  
• Base simple payback at $60B: ~240 years before financing  
• Upside case works only with low capex, high traffic, high tariffs, and limited overruns  
Speaker notes: The problem is not only volume; it is volume that can pay. High-value containers can pay more but lack scale, while bulk cargo has scale but cannot tolerate high tolls.

--- Slide 7: Logistics and Trade Impact ---
• Fixed link improves Caspian reliability but solves only one corridor segment  
• Remaining bottlenecks: Kazakhstan rail, Alat/Kuryk/Aktau handling, customs, BTK, Georgia–Türkiye links  
• Middle Corridor is a resilience and premium-speed route, not full Suez replacement  
• Strongest cargo fit: high-value, time-sensitive, sanctions-compliant freight  
• Bulk cargo helps tonnage but is tariff-sensitive  
Speaker notes: A bridge or tunnel would reduce Caspian crossing uncertainty, but the corridor is only as strong as its weakest link. Without system-wide upgrades, congestion simply moves elsewhere.

--- Slide 8: Legal, Environmental, and Technical Risks ---
• 2018 Caspian Convention is not a turnkey approval regime  
• AZ–KZ treaty needed for alignment, jurisdiction, customs, tariffs, safety, liability  
• Consultation needed with Russia, Iran, Turkmenistan on navigation, ecology, security impacts  
• EIA risks: Caspian seals, sturgeon, wetlands, contaminated sediments, hydrocarbon infrastructure  
• Sea-level decline supports port adaptation but adds shoreline and design uncertainty  
Speaker notes: These risks are not side issues; they are bankability gates. Lenders and MDBs will require legal certainty, environmental clearance, and regional consultation before considering financing.

--- Slide 9: Financing and JV Assessment ---
• Tariff-funded fixed-link JV is not profitable under base-case assumptions  
• Equal JV ownership does not mean equal affordability: Azerbaijan has much smaller GDP  
• PPP/MDB/BRI finance cannot remove core demand and fiscal risks  
• Private capital is better suited to terminals, logistics zones, vessels, digital systems, O&M  
• Future fixed-link SPV should be ring-fenced and created only after stage gates  
Speaker notes: We support Azerbaijan–Kazakhstan cooperation, but with the right mandate. The bilateral entity should build the corridor market, not carry $35–90B of construction debt today.

--- Slide 10: Preferred Alternative — Maritime Bridge Program ---
• $3–7B phased program during 2026–2035  
• Fleet: rail ferries, Ro-Ro vessels, container-capable services, scheduled sailings  
• Ports: Alat, Kuryk, Aktau berth upgrades, dredging, movable ramps, sea-level adaptation  
• Systems: digital customs, unified tariffs, booking/tracking, through-bill-of-lading  
• Development: logistics zones, bonded warehousing, cold chain, private terminal concessions  
Speaker notes: This is not “do nothing” and not “ferries forever.” It is a proactive, scalable corridor modernization program that builds demand and reliability before irreversible megaproject spending.

--- Slide 11: Fixed Link vs Maritime Bridge vs Status Quo ---
• Fixed link now: high strategic value, weak bankability, high fiscal/legal/environmental risk  
• Maritime Bridge: high strategic value, moderate/strong bankability, faster implementation  
• Status quo: low cost but inadequate reliability and capacity  
• Maritime Bridge can generate benefits before 2035; fixed link likely post-2040  
• Preferred path: invest now in modular assets; preserve fixed-link optionality  
Speaker notes: The real choice is not bridge versus no bridge. It is whether to spend tens of billions now, or first build a scalable system that proves demand and keeps future options open.

--- Slide 12: Stage-Gate Framework ---
• Gate 1: 2026 — create Corridor Company and launch Maritime Bridge Program  
• Gate 2: full feasibility only after sustained 20–30 Mt/year and no legal/EIA fatal flaw  
• Gate 3: construction only after 50–60 Mt/year fixed-link-payable demand and take-or-pay contracts  
• Gate 3 also requires P90 capex ≤ ~$35–40B, downstream capacity, clean EIA/legal clearance  
• Public-subsidy exception only if governments prove ~$1–2B/year wider benefits  
Speaker notes: We recommend evidence-based gates, not political momentum. Traffic of 20–30 Mt justifies deeper study, not construction.

--- Slide 13: First 24-Month Action Plan ---
• Establish asset-light Trans-Caspian Corridor Company  
• Launch unified booking, tracking, tariff, and KPI platform  
• Pilot guaranteed scheduled ferry/Ro-Ro/rail-ferry services  
• Negotiate anchor-user capacity contracts with railways, exporters, and logistics firms  
• Start MDB-supervised fixed-link pre-feasibility, legal scoping, and environmental baseline  
Speaker notes: These actions are practical and financeable immediately. They also create the market evidence needed to decide later whether a fixed link deserves serious consideration.

--- Slide 14: Final Conclusion ---
• Trans-Caspian connectivity is worth pursuing urgently  
• A 300–370 km fixed link is not worth building now via tariff-funded JV  
• Best near-term investment: Corridor Company + Maritime Bridge Program  
• Fixed link remains a long-term option after demand, capex, legal, fiscal, and EIA proof  
• Strategic prize: reliable, competitive, sanctions-resilient Middle Corridor  
Speaker notes: Our final message is disciplined ambition. Build the Middle Corridor system now; do not overbuild the Caspian crossing before fixed-link-payable demand, corridor-wide capacity, affordable capex, legal certainty, Caspian consultation, and environmental clearance are proven.
