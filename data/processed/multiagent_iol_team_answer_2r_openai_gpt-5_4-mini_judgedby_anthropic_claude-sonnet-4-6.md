(a) **How ancient fanqie can be used in modern Cantonese**

Fanqie gives the pronunciation of a character by using two known characters:

- the **first** gives the **initial consonant**,
- the **second** gives the **rhyme and tone**.

In modern Cantonese, one can use an ancient fanqie by:

1. taking the Cantonese initial of the first character;
2. taking the Cantonese final of the second character;
3. adjusting for historical sound changes:
   - voiced Middle Chinese initials have split according to tone;
   - some tones changed pitch/register;
   - some initials changed aspiration or voicing.

So the old transcription still works if we interpret it through the regular sound correspondences.

(b) **How fanqie was designed to work at the time of Guangyun**

At the time of Guangyun, fanqie was intended as a simple phonetic notation:

- the first character had the **same initial** as the target character;
- the second character had the **same final and tone** as the target character;
- both chosen characters were assumed to be well known already.

Thus, reading the two characters in sequence gave the pronunciation of the target character.

(d) **How the three tones in this problem evolved into the six tones of Cantonese**

Classical Chinese had four tone categories:

- level,
- rising,
- departing,
- entering.

In Cantonese, the **level** and **departing** categories split according to whether the original initial was voiceless or voiced, producing a **yin/yang split**. This yields six non-entering tone classes; entering syllables split in parallel but are traditionally treated separately because they end in stop codas.

So Cantonese six-tone system = old tone categories + split by initial voicing.
