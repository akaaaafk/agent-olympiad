**Task A.3 — Photochemical reduction of indophenol**

**(a)** DPIP is blue in the oxidised form and becomes colourless when reduced.

**(b)** In solution 1, **ascorbic acid reduces DPIP chemically**, so the blue colour should fade even without light.

**(c)** In solution 2, **phosphoric acid alone does not reduce DPIP**; it mainly changes the pH, so no true bleaching is expected from reduction.

**(d)** For the qualitative test:
- **unboiled chloroplasts + light**: DPIP should fade/bleach
- **unboiled chloroplasts + dark**: little or no change
- **boiled chloroplasts + light or dark**: little or no change
- **chlorophyllin + light**: may show some light-dependent change, but it is not expected to behave like intact chloroplasts

**(e)** The correct qualitative conclusion is that **intact, unboiled chloroplasts in light are required for efficient photochemical reduction of DPIP**.

**(f)** For the quantitative experiment, the best wavelength to monitor is near the absorption maximum of oxidised DPIP, i.e. **about 600–610 nm**.

**(g)** As DPIP is reduced, its absorbance at that wavelength should **decrease with time**.

**(h)** The rate of photochemical reduction is obtained from the **slope of absorbance vs time**:
- rate = **negative gradient**
- report as either \(\Delta A / \Delta t\) or as the magnitude of the decrease per minute

**(i)** For both samples E and F, the rates should be similar if the experiment is reproducible.
